Overview

Engine: Unity    Responsibilities: Programming, Puzzle/Level Design

"Grounded" is a virtual reality puzzle game where players are in the shoes of an old, amnesiac farmer who awakens to an audio log of himself explaining the existence of a runaway experiment. Rediscovering the possessions of his now unfamiliar home, the farmer realizes he is trapped and that the only way out is by solving a series of puzzles origianlly designed to contain the beast that is now lurking somewhere near him.

Players spawn next to a phonograph that automatically plays the first audio log. Audio logs can be replayed if grabbed and reinserted
(Objects in picture are lighter than they appear).

The project was developed over the course of two months with a friend, who focused on asset building and modeling while I handled programming the mechanics, event triggers, and object interactions. We chose virtual reality as the optimal medium for immersing our audience to environmental storytelling, which complemented our desire to implement a more hands-on approach to our puzzles.

Four puzzles exist in the game and each represent a chapter in the story as completing one rewards players with audio logs or clues for the next one. When players are not solving puzzles, they are discovering narrative artifacts placed all around the level to give them a better understanding of what is happening around them. Finding this balance between open-world exploration and narrative progress was an essential feat for development and definitely gave us a stronger understanding of our players through weekly playtest sessions.

Controls

Left Joystick - Movement
Right Joystick - Head turning
Right Trigger - Grab object (Use again to drop)

Puzzle Design

Designing the puzzles was the most strenuous task as we were juggling the balance of effective puzzle design and a narrative that seamlessly follows that progression. We designed various events to trigger depending on which puzzle was solved to visually and audibly cue narrative progression to the player. Because we were constrained to work with a linear story, we had to work around players accidentally solving certain puzzles out of order by placing significant puzzle fragments in areas where it would be impossible to discover earlier.

Wheel Combination Lock

For the "Padlock + Painting" puzzle, for example, paintings were scattered all around the house, each with a hidden number that spell out a combination for a lock on a door when put together. Unlocking this door triggers a climactic point in the story, so we had two of the paintings locked away in a room that gets unlocked from an earlier puzzle to prevent players from missing the gradual narrative progression. Several red herrings were also placed to make finding this correlation rather difficult. This didn't solve the problem of players just going through every single combination on the padlock to eventually unlock it and we left it that way for a good reason.

SPOILER: Side by side map view of all puzzles and puzzle pieces, each color-coded to their puzzle

If you carefully observe where each puzzle's pieces are placed in the map, you will notice that no matter where you path, there is always enough information to tease the existence of a puzzle but not enough to offer a viable solution. Solving the padlock first leads you to a bookcase which can immediately be solved if you realize to place the book in it to reveal the room with a flamethrower. However, you will not be able to make it past the end-game door as there is another lock that needs to be opened. This prompts the existence of a key but does not tell you where it is, saving the satisfying "AHA" moment as a reward for later when discovery of a hammer redirects your attention to a fragile vase from the room you passed by earlier. It was our creed that "good puzzles are about obfuscation, not obscuration". To make a puzzle rewarding, enough information needs to be relayed to reward those who figure it out, not to those who "found the key" or "found the book". Placing a key on the table to a lock in front of is not a satisfying puzzle, but using a padlock of numbers you pieced together from a series of unusually similar paintings is.

Narrative Approach

Though not a popular argument, I believe that there are some aspects of games that make them unreliable mediums for stories. A person's story, once gamified, will not pan out the same for every reader in control and, thus, produces a slightly different narrative each time. What we allow for our stories to compensate for this lack of linearity is the ability to time-travel and alter these circumstances while still abiding by the laws of that universe. Because players are free to explore and free to solve puzzles in any order they want, the narrative is simplified to fragments - triggers and environmental pieces that provide an experience that isn't always intended to players. And that's the price that we had to pay to sustain a proactive puzzle experience that doesn't funnel solutions through a mere "A to B, B to C" progression.

Thus, event triggers that activated subtle pieces of the narrative upon puzzle completion are designed to be in no chronological order and are expressive forms of the story. How we decided to project our story is by slowly introducing the experiment the farmer had created. Incorporating some methods of horror, we wanted to make players feel uneased by making the house as eery as possible. This included the lack of windows, dim lighting, very low-pitched ambience, and a claustrophobic house design that has exploration eventually come to a dead-end after a dedicated amount of time. As for the presence of life, we went for the "breathing house" feel where occasional noises will prompt players to look over their shoulder and subtle changes in the environment will have players questioning their sanity. The more triggers activated, the more these sorts of things would happen. However, we wanted to ensure that players ended the game satisfied, so we decided not to put in any jump-scares and, instead, hold the feeling of the potential existence of one which was honestly more effective if anything. In the end, we had fun doing this and we learned that fear functions in very different ways.

Problems with VR and my future with it

One of the biggest problems that we failed to overlook was how much motion sickness affects quality gameplay experiences. During most playtest sessions, there were people that were not able to get passed the second or third puzzle due to the amount of time it took being plugged in and the lower threshold for newer VR users. A lot of this problem had to do with the fluctuating frame rates as we were using weaker machines, and the fact that movement had to be simulated by joystick rather than the player's actual feet. The amount of wires needed to use the Oculus Rift also made it dangerous for users to use their actual heads to turn around which exacerbated the issue when we decided to use the right joystick for head turning as the replacement. There were many ways we could've suppressed the problem, but ultimately there wasn't a viable solution that would make not using virtual reality at all a reasonable one. There are many iterations that I'd like to try for VR controls in the future should the time come where I could get my hands on one again. But until then, I've used this experience as a learning lesson that user experience starts the moment they touch the controller.
